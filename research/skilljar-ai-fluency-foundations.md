## Course Overview

This free course teaches "practical skills for effective, efficient, ethical, and safe AI interaction." It introduces the **AI Fluency Framework**, a descriptive and normative model of Human-AI interaction built on four core competencies known as the **4D Framework**: Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence.

AI Fluency is defined as "the ability to work effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely within emerging modalities (automation, agency, and augmentation) of Human-AI interaction."

Establishes foundational knowledge by introducing generative AI systems and explaining why developing AI fluency matters for effective collaboration. Covers both newcomers and experienced AI practitioners. Emphasizes that AI fluency is not just about how to use AI but understanding when and why.

## Lesson 2: The AI Fluency Framework

### Why Do We Need AI Fluency?

AI fluency matters because Gen AI adoption is accelerating across professional domains. The framework provides a structured approach to human-AI interaction that goes beyond ad-hoc usage.

### The 4D Framework

The four core competencies:

1. **Delegation** -- Setting goals and deciding whether, when, and how to engage with AI
2. **Description** -- Effectively describing goals to prompt useful AI behaviors and outputs
3. **Discernment** -- Accurately assessing the usefulness of AI outputs and behaviors
4. **Diligence** -- Taking responsibility for what we do with AI and how we do it

These elements function as an **interconnected cycle** rather than isolated sequential steps. Each D corresponds to a quality of AI interaction:
- Delegation -> Effective and Efficient
- Description -> Effective
- Discernment -> Effective
- Diligence -> Ethical and Safe

## Lesson 3: Deep Dive 1 -- What is Generative AI?

### Generative AI Fundamentals

Generative AI = "systems that create new content, enabled by advancements in algorithms, data availability, and computing power."

**Key technical concepts covered:**
- **Transformer architecture** -- the breakthrough architecture powering modern LLMs
- **Pre-training** -- learning patterns from vast text corpora
- **Fine-tuning** -- adapting pre-trained models for specific tasks
- **Deployment** -- making models available for use
- **Context windows** -- the amount of text a model can process at once
- **Emergent capabilities** -- abilities that arise from scale (reasoning, coding, analysis)
- **Token-by-token generation** -- models generate text one token at a time, predicting the most likely next token

### Capabilities & Limitations

**Capabilities:**
- Natural language understanding and generation
- Reasoning and analysis
- Code generation
- Creative content
- Translation and summarization
- Multi-turn conversation

**Limitations:**
- Knowledge cutoffs (training data has a date boundary)
- Hallucinations (generating plausible but incorrect information)
- No real-time access to information (without tools)
- Context window limits
- Potential for bias from training data
- Cannot truly "understand" in the human sense

## Three AI Work Models (Interaction Modalities)

### Automation
AI independently executes human-defined tasks based on direct instructions. Best for repetitive, time-consuming, or data-intensive work (emails, summaries, coding). "AI automation is like pressing a button on a microwave."

### Augmentation
Humans and AI collaboratively define and execute tasks iteratively toward shared objectives. Enhances human creativity rather than replacing it (writing, research, complex coding). "AI augmentation is like using a food processor, where you are fully in control."

### Agency
Humans configure AI systems to perform future tasks independently. Requires deep understanding of AI capabilities and constraints (chatbots, tutors, game characters). Works "like hiring a chef" to execute detailed specifications.

## Lesson 4: Delegation -- A Closer Look

### Definition
Delegation is about identifying when and how to deploy AI tools within workflows. It is about "leverage and amplification, not mere automation."

### Three Sub-Components

#### 1. Goal/Task Awareness (also called Problem Awareness)
- Envisioning project goals before opening an AI chat
- Understanding the scope of the work
- Defining criteria for success -- what a "good" outcome looks like
- Deconstructing tasks into AI, human, and collaborative elements

#### 2. Platform Awareness
- Having a realistic understanding of what AI systems can and cannot do
- Understanding tool capabilities and limitations
- Evaluating platforms against project needs
- The model itself may push back if given too much responsibility

#### 3. Task Delegation
- Balancing AI and human strengths
- Assigning tasks optimally across modalities (automation, augmentation, agency)
- Strategic process of dividing work between humans and AI
- Moving beyond ad-hoc task offloading to thoughtful, strategic approach

### Project Planning and Delegation
- Identify sub-tasks within a project
- Assess which tool/approach is best for each
- Determine optimal task assignment (human vs. AI vs. collaborative)
- Key principle: "using AI not to replace capability but to amplify it"

### Delegation Exercises
The course provides exercises and questions to help users clarify their goals and make informed decisions about delegating tasks between themselves and AI systems.

## Lesson 5: Description -- A Closer Look

### Definition
Description is about communicating creative visions effectively to AI systems through clear prompts and directives. It reframes prompting as a discipline of clarity, similar to writing product requirements.

### Three Sub-Components

#### 1. Product Description
- Articulating desired output characteristics in AI-understandable terms
- Specifying format, length, tone, style
- Defining the end product clearly

#### 2. Process Description
- Engaging in iterative dialogue with AI
- Breaking complex tasks into manageable prompts
- Providing step-by-step instructions when needed

#### 3. Performance Description
- Defining how AI systems should behave and interact
- Setting behavioral constraints
- Specifying roles and personas

## Lesson 6: Deep Dive 2 -- Effective Prompting Techniques

**Six Core Prompting Techniques:**

1. **Context provision** -- Providing background information and relevant details
2. **Examples** -- Showing the AI what good output looks like (few-shot prompting)
3. **Constraints** -- Setting boundaries on length, format, tone, scope
4. **Step-by-step reasoning** -- Asking AI to break down its thinking process
5. **Asking AI to "think first"** -- Having the model reason before answering
6. **Defining tone or role** -- Establishing a persona or communication style

**Key Insight:** Treating prompts with the same rigor as PRDs (Product Requirements Documents) improves clarity across all communication with AI systems.

## Lesson 7: Discernment -- A Closer Look

### Definition
Discernment is about critically evaluating AI-generated outputs and refining collaborative processes. It involves embedding quality control into every step.

### Three Sub-Components

#### 1. Product Discernment
- Assessing output quality against success criteria
- Identifying strengths and weaknesses in AI output
- Checking for accuracy, logical consistency, and goal alignment

#### 2. Process Discernment
- Evaluating the effectiveness of the human-AI collaboration itself
- Identifying optimization opportunities
- Determining if the prompting approach needs adjustment

#### 3. Performance Discernment
- Assessing how well AI systems behave independently
- Gathering user feedback for refinement
- Evaluating whether the AI's "character" matches expectations

### The Description-Discernment Loop

A core concept in the framework: the iterative cycle of:
1. **Describe** -- Provide clear instructions/prompts to AI
2. **Evaluate** -- Critically assess the output (Discernment)
3. **Refine** -- Improve the prompt based on evaluation
4. **Repeat** -- Continue until output meets quality standards

This loop prevents "AI features that feel magical once but degrade over time." It requires:
- Testing model outputs
- Comparing multiple responses
- Understanding when model reasoning fails
- Multiple refinement cycles may be needed

## Lesson 8: Diligence -- A Closer Look

### Definition
Diligence is about assuming responsibility for AI-assisted products through ethical practice, accountability, and transparency. It covers practicing transparency and ethics.

### Three Sub-Components

#### 1. Creation Diligence
- Responsible tool use
- Applying ethical principles
- Bias mitigation in AI outputs
- Accuracy concerns and fact-checking

#### 2. Transparency Diligence
- Clearly communicating AI involvement in work products
- Meeting audience and legal expectations for disclosure
- Documenting when AI contributes to work
- Tracking model versions used
- Disclosing collaboration when appropriate

#### 3. Deployment Diligence
- Fact-checking AI-generated content
- Testing before release
- Validating claims
- Managing risks
- Privacy considerations

### Anthropic's Own Practice
Anthropic disclosed that Claude 3.7 helped draft course materials under full human supervision -- modeling the transparency they teach.

### Key Principle
Diligence builds "earned trust" rather than "compliance theater." It means treating safety, privacy, and interpretability as features, not afterthoughts.

## Key Takeaways

1. **AI Fluency requires understanding when and why to use AI, not just how**
2. **The 4D Framework is platform-agnostic** -- works with any AI system
3. **Design for human + AI workflows as collaborative teams**
4. **The Description-Discernment loop is central** -- iterative refinement drives quality
5. **Treat prompts with engineering rigor** -- like PRDs or engineering specs
6. **Ethics (Diligence) is fundamental, not peripheral** -- integrated throughout
7. **Three modalities** (automation, augmentation, agency) require different delegation strategies
8. **AI delegation is about leverage and amplification, not replacement**
